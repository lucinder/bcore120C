    In class on Tuesday, we explored the myth of Hades and Persephone in brief, discussion its relations to the natural cycle of seasons and the traditional coming-of-age tale, but we only touched on the symbolism of the pomegranate. Pomegranates as a fruit tend to find themselves in the position of easy physical metaphors due to a number of unique features: the juicy, edible part of a pomegranate is the rind around its seeds, which are contained within the bulbous fruit. In Ancient Greece, pomegranates were primarily known as the “fruit of the dead” (Graves 1992), yet in later cultures, they were more frequently presented as symbols of fertility (Hudson, 2017). The word pomegranate itself comes from the old French pome grenate, which itself derives from the latin pōmum grānātum, literally meaning “seedy apple”. Like apples, pomegranates are one of the proposed real-life equivalents of the biblical “forbidden fruit,” which draws a parallel between the myth of Adam and Eve and Hades and Persephone- the eating of the fruit is seen as an irreversible decision, perhaps interpretable as a corruption of one’s innocence. In Genesis, Eve is first to devour the fruit before Adam, which further supports the potential of influence of the myth of Persephone on this biblical story.

    As we go on in the quarter, I hope I can talk more about mythological parallels like this between cultures, as these short examples are the foundations of a larger global picture of migration and cultural mixing that lead to a lot of the different sects of modern religion we see today.
